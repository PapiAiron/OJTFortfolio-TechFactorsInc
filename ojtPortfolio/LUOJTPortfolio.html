04 / INTRODUCTION

This portfolio documents our enriching internship experience at TechFactors Inc., a leading technology company specializing in educational robotics and equipment management. Over the course of 6 weeks, we immersed ourselves in a professional environment, gaining valuable insights into software development, project management, and industry best practices.

During our internship, we developed an Equipment Audit System as our culminating project, applying theoretical knowledge to real-world challenges. This experience honed our technical skills and fostered our ability to collaborate, adapt to evolving requirements, and deliver solutions that meet operational needs.

The internship reinforced the importance of the industry–academe partnership in bridging the gap between education and employment, equipping us with the competencies necessary to thrive in the competitive tech landscape.

05 /MBOT2 ASSEMBLY & CODING
WEEK 01 — FEB 23–26
Assembly
Calibration
Coding
Testing
Narrative

The first week focused on getting acquainted with the next generation of educational robotics. I was tasked with the physical assembly and software calibration of the mBot2. Unlike standard kits, the mBot2 requires a more precise touch to ensure all CyberPi sensors are correctly seated. Once complete, I moved into the programming phase using Makeblock's ecosystem, eventually syncing the robot with a Bluetooth controller for manual override testing.

Experiences & Lessons

I learned that hardware is only as good as its calibration. Assembling the mBot2 taught me the importance of cable management in robotics to prevent mechanical interference. On the coding side, I discovered how to map Bluetooth inputs to specific motor speeds, deepening my understanding of real-time remote-control system logic.

06 /MBOT1 & PICO W PROGRAMMING
WEEK 02 — MAR 2–13
mBot1
Pico W
Thonny IDE
Arduino IDE
Narrative

This week involved significant context-switching between different microcontrollers. I worked on the mBot1, focusing on its Arduino-based foundation, then shifted to the Raspberry Pi Pico W. This was a test of adaptability — jumping between block-based/Python logic of the mBots and the C/C++ environment required for the Pico W and Arduino IDE.

Experiences & Lessons

The biggest takeaway was learning to navigate Thonny and Arduino IDE simultaneously. I learned to read schematics for the Pico W to ensure correct pinouts. This week solidified my understanding of how Python vs. C++ are each optimized for different hardware constraints and performance requirements.

WEEK 2 — 06 / 11mBot1 · Raspberry Pi Pico W · Thonny · Arduino IDE
07 /INITIAL AUDIT SYSTEM PRESENTATION
WEEK 03 — MAR 16–19
Presentation
Prototype
Workshop
Narrative

We officially shifted from hardware to software this week. Our team presented the initial concept and HTML prototype of the Equipment Auditing System to department heads. This high-stakes meeting saw us defend our choice of React + Vite and TypeScript. The session became a collaborative workshop outlining specific needs such as distinct access levels for the RnD and Academy teams.

Experiences & Lessons

I learned that "System Design" is a conversation, not just instructions. I gained experience translating non-technical requirements — like "we need to monitor but not edit" — into technical logic such as Read-Only Admin Access Levels. This highlighted the value of a flexible frontend architecture that accommodates feature creep early in the development cycle.

08 /NEW ERA UNIVERSITY DEPLOYMENT
WEEK 04 — MAR 23–27
Deployment
Demo
Students
Narrative

The team traveled to New Era University at Commonwealth, Quezon City, for a full-scale deployment and demo. We showcased mBot2 and Arduino projects to both elementary and high school students. Beyond demonstrating the robots, I explained the logic behind the projects and participated as a judge for their application development competition — requiring constant on-the-fly troubleshooting.

Experiences & Lessons

This week taught me the "soft skills" of engineering. It's one thing to make a robot work in a quiet lab; another to keep it running in a crowded room of excited students. I learned to troubleshoot connectivity issues on the fly and to simplify complex technical concepts for younger audiences. Exhausting, but incredibly rewarding.

WEEK 04 — MAR 23–27

Narrative (cont.)

Beyond the robotics showcase, we participated in judging an application development competition for high school students. Evaluating their projects required balancing encouragement with constructive critique — applying rubrics fairly and giving students actionable feedback. This role reversal from student to evaluator gave us a unique perspective on the tech education pipeline.

Key Takeaways

Seeing technology in the hands of end-users was transformative. Young students' curiosity and enthusiasm reinforced why building good, accessible tech matters. The ability to contextualize engineering concepts for a non-technical audience is a professional skill as critical as writing clean code.

10 /FINAL PRESENTATION — TALA.SYS
WEEK 06 — APR 10–17
Final Demo
System Review
Narrative

The internship culminated in the final presentation of our web application, officially titled TALA.sys. We demonstrated the full end-to-end workflow: from manual account creation in Firebase to role-specific auditing dashboards built with React and TypeScript. Department heads reviewed the progress, tested the access logic, and expressed great satisfaction with the system's stability and Glassmorphism design language.

Experiences & Lessons

I learned that the last 10% of a project — polishing, bug fixes, and UI tweaks — is what makes it feel production-ready. Seeing the department heads' positive reaction taught me that UX is just as important as backend logic. The core system was successfully validated for deployment, with only minor final adjustments noted.
